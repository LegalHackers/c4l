In April 2018, Legal Hackers and the LIT Lab at Suffolk Law School convened coding for lawyers instructors from around the United States for its first Coding for Lawyers Summit. Today we are publishing our draft coding for lawyers course taxonomy and list of courses and syllabuses with a request for feedback and collaborators to improve and update the list (submit yours here!).

Background

The purpose of the Coding for Lawyers Summit was “to explore the purposes, methods, and future of [“coding for lawyers”] courses, with an overarching goal of being descriptive, rather than prescriptive.” To that end, the participants shared their own approaches to teaching coding for lawyers, discussed challenges and opportunities, and worked collaboratively to develop shared resources to better enable others to build and host their own coding for lawyers courses.

The meeting included representatives from several schools -- American University Washington College of Law, Brooklyn Law School, Chicago-Kent IIT School of Law, Georgetown University Law Center, Harvard Law School, New York University, Suffolk Law School, the University of Baltimore, the University of Ottawa's Law School, and Vermont Law School -- and representatives of two software platforms for lawyers, Docassemble and Legalese. While the participants largely represented North American law schools, our intent is to incorporate global perspectives going forward.

The Summit included a round of introductions and course overviews, followed by three breakout groups: (1) what to teach coding for lawyers, (2) how to teach coding for lawyers, and (3) to whom to teach coding for lawyers. Questions addressed included:

  • Do we need different courses for different audiences (e.g., lawyers, law students, CS students, etc)? What about advanced courses?
  • What is the appropriate scope for an introductory course? What is "coding"/"computer programming"?
  • What is the purpose of coding for lawyers courses? Writing/reading programs? Understanding technology?
  • What skills/tools should we be teaching?
  • How should students be evaluated?
  • To what extent should courses include an ethical or policy component?
  • How can these courses coordinate with existing communities (Code for America, Legal Hackers etc.)
  • How can the courses promote diversity and inclusion?

Toward the end of the day, the group reconvened to collaborate on a shared mission statement:

We are building a diverse global community of instructors and students who teach coding for lawyers courses with the goal of developing and sharing free syllabuses, resources, and best practices. If you teach coding for lawyers and are interested in joining the community, please get in touch!

Following the Summit, participants continued to work on their respective tasks. Jack Cushman of Harvard, Jeanne Eicks of Vermont Law School, and Jess Garson of NYU hosted a workshop on teaching coding for lawyers at the Fourth Annual Legal Hackers International Summit. And David Colarusso and his students updated the course taxonomy and prepared a catalogue of existing courses.

Key Themes from the Summit

Several themes emerged from the summit and follow-up activity:

(1) “Coding for Lawyers” is not a monolith

Participants revealed a wide array of courses that could reasonably fall under the “coding for lawyers” banner, each with its own learning objectives, methods, and requirements. For example, while Georgetown’s “Computer Programming for Lawyers” course is designed to teach lawyers how to program in Python (“pure coding for lawyers”), Harvard’s Coding for Lawyers course is designed to teach legal problem-solving and doctrine through coding exercises (“law-through-code”). Some courses are project-based, some are exercise-based, some are clinical (serving clients), and some combine all three. Some courses don’t include a hands-on component at all, focused instead on technology awareness and tool use.

(2) There is strong student demand for these courses (but capacity challenges)

The experience of instructors of coding for lawyers courses reveals a strong student demand to develop skills that the students view as useful for practice. By the end of this year, Georgetown expects that 30 percent of graduating 3Ls will have at least some coding experience.

At the same time, many of the more advanced coding for lawyers courses require instructors to have coding skills themselves, or to bring in someone who has them. As a result, many schools -- particularly those without a computer science department -- may struggle to launch their own courses.

(3) Schools should ensure diversity and inclusion in any coding for lawyers course

Participants agreed that inclusivity and diversity are critical to the success of any coding for lawyers class. Some participants noted that first-time coding for lawyers courses drew only male attendees. Participants offered several suggestions to promote inclusivity, including

  1. ensuring diverse teaching teams,
  2. highlighting social justice aspects of the courses to broaden appeal, and
  3. intentionally recruiting and encouraging diverse students to take the course

What’s Next? (We Need Your Help!)

While the first Coding for Lawyers summit was a fun and engaging experiment, it hardly scratched the surface of global courses focused on teaching law students and lawyers technology skills. Further, since the summit, coding for lawyers courses and interest in such courses have taken off around the world, outstripping our ability to create a single mater document.

As a result, we believe that a collaborative approach is the best course of action. To that end, please take a look at our draft taxonomy and course list and let us know:

  • Did we get the taxonomy right?
  • What courses are we missing in our course list?
  • How might we develop open course materials?
  • Would there be value in a global virtual convening?

We look forward to hearing from you!
